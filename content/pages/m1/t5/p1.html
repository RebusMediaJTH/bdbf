Meet Andre

He’s smart, charming, well-spoken, and he has a lovely family who’s involved in the community.

What you don’t see, is that Andre’s tried over 500 times to gain access to the Australia Post network to take and sell highly confidential customer information, including credit card details. Breaking into our network isn’t easy, so Andre’s looking for a simpler way — like sending an email to trick someone into gaining him access.

You’re the first line of defence against people like Andre. So, what can you do?

Scroll down to find out.

Be aware

Let’s take a look at all the information Andre is able to get from your social media.

Select each item to learn more.

Your location

Complete

When you post about your location or ‘check in’ somewhere, cyber criminals can use this location information to send targeted phishing emails or to call you, impersonating as known entities such as phone companies or internet providers.

Public privacy settings

Complete

Cyber criminals love when profiles are set to Public. We get excited online and provide so much personal information freely, that the cyber criminal can easily access this to build a profile of us.

Access to photos

Complete

You might think you’re just posting a selfie you took at work, but you might be freely sharing enough personal and business information for a cyber criminal to launch a targeted scam email.

Be aware of what you post online and how it may be used by cyber criminals.

Without you knowing, cyber criminals could be collecting your information to use for malicious attacks.

Social engineering

It isn’t just online tactics that cyber criminals use to try and gain access to our networks. Consider the following real-life scenario.

You receive a call instore. The woman on the line says she is from Telstra and tells you that due to high volumes of emails and social media messages being sent through the AP network, the internet service will be disconnected. She starts walking you through steps at your computer to check the network activity.

What do you do?

Select your answer, then select Submit.

  • Complete the steps as instructed
  • Tell her to call Head Office and give her the number
  • Ask for her full name and Telstra ID
  • Transfer her through to the Helpdesk

Good thinking. Once you ask for details, the scammer will likely end the call. Always remain alert — just because they give you details, doesn’t mean they’re genuine. Tell the caller you’ll contact our Helpdesk and pass the information on for their follow-up, then end the call and escalate the incident immediately.

Not quite. It’s best to ask some questions first. If the caller is a scammer, they’re unlikely to give you information and may end the call themselves. Transferring them or asking them to call another number may result in them targeting another unsuspecting team member.

Threats are real people who impersonate real service providers.
