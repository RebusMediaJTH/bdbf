Everyone should be able to come to work and be treated with courtesy, respect and dignity. Australia Post will not tolerate harassment, discrimination or bullying in the workplace. We also make it easy for you to safely report problems without being victimised.

We all have the responsibility to call out and prevent unacceptable behaviour.

If you participate in behaviour that constitutes harassment, discrimination or bullying you may be subject to disciplinary action and you could lose your job. This may occur even if you didn’t intend to harass, bully or discriminate.

What is harassment?

You’re more than likely harassing someone if:

  • you make fun of their disability, sex, race, colour, religion or any other traits
  • the other person doesn’t like it and doesn’t return it
  • it offends, insults, humiliates, intimidates or embarrasses, and
  • it’s obvious to most people that what you said or did would be offensive, humiliating or intimidating.

What is discrimination?

You’re more than likely discriminating if you’re treating a person or group of people unfairly or less favourably than others because of their sex, race, disability, age or some other trait. It’s important to note that unreasonable discriminating behaviours aren’t just limited to this list.

Discrimination can even occur if you don’t intend to discriminate.

Discrimination can be direct or indirect. Since you’ve only been given information about Andrea and Gabrielle’s personal circumstances, making your rostering decision based solely on this would be a form of discrimination. To make a fair decision, you’d need to consider things such as who’s worked more holiday periods, who’s taken more leave in the past twelve months, or who submitted their leave request first.

What is Bullying?

You’re more than likely bullying someone or a group of people if you:

  • use abusive, insulting or offensive language
  • spread misinformation or rumours
  • use frightening behaviour
  • criticise via yelling
  • make jokes

And it:

  • happens more than once
  • is obvious to most people that you’re victimising, humiliating or threatening.

It’s not bullying if a supervisor or manager directs someone’s work or conducts a performance or behaviour conversation with someone — that’s their job. As long as the supervisor or manager communicates the directive in line with the minimum behavioural expectations outlined in Our Ethics, they are doing things the right way.
