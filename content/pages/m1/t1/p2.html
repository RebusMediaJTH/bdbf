What is harassment?

You're more than likely harassing someone if:

  • You make fun of their disability, sex, race, colour or any of the other traits listed below.
    +
  • The other person doesn't like it and doesn't return it.
    +
  • It offends, insults, humiliates, intimidates or embarrasses the other person.
    +
  • It's pretty obvious to most people that what you said or did would be offensive, humiliating or intimidating.

What is sexual harassment?

Sexual harassment can be something that's said, done, sent, displayed, or written of a sexual nature AND is an unwelcome:

  • Sexual advance
    and/or
  • Request for sexual favour
    and/or
  • Other unwelcome behaviour of a sexual nature.

These are just some examples:

  • Unwelcome physical conduct
  • Sexual or sexist jokes
  • Sexual innuendo or propositioning
  • Sexual assault
  • Displays of sexually explicit material (including via email or mobile phones)
  • Inappropriate gifts

What is discrimination?

You're more than likely discriminating if you're treating a person or a group of people unfairly or less favourably than others because of their sex, race, disability, age or some other trait, rather than according to their individual skills, abilities and talents.

It can even occur if you don't intend to discriminate!

For example, everybody needs to attend a compulsory two-day conference interstate. Single parents with small children without any support would be discriminated against in this case.

This is called indirect discrimination.

What is bullying?

You're more than likely bullying someone or a group of people if you're:

  • Using abusive, insulting or offensive language
  • Spreading misinformation or rumours
  • Using frightening behaviour
  • Criticising via yelling
  • Making practical jokes
    +
  • It happens repeatedly (i.e. not once-off)
    +
  • It's pretty obvious to most people that what you're saying or doing is victimising, humiliating or threatening

Note that unreasonable, bullying behaviours aren't just limited to the list above.

What is victimisation?

You're victimising someone if you're treating them unfairly, or acting harshly towards them, because:

  • They've complained or could complain, or
  • They've been involved in some way with a complaint of harassment, sexual harassment, discrimination or bullying.

How can you know if a person's behaviour is harassment or bullying?

It isn't always easy to be sure if a particular situation is an example of harassment or bullying. To help you work it out we have the Three-Part Test and the AB Test.

The Three-Part Test

Use the Three-Part Test to decide if the behaviour could be harassment.

  • 1.
    Is it behaviour that is directed at a person's disability, sex, race, colour or other traits listed above?

    Yes

    +

  • 2.
    Is it behaviour that another person doesn't like and doesn't return?

    Yes

    +

  • 3.
    Is it behaviour that offends, insults, humiliates, intimidates or embarrasses the person? Would it be pretty obvious to most people that what was said or done would be offensive, humiliating or intimidating?

    Yes

    =

    If the answer to ALL of these questions is Yes, then the situation could be harassment.

The AB Test for Bullying

Use the AB Test to decide if the behaviour could be bullying.

  • A
    Is the unreasonable behaviour (use of abusive language, criticising via yelling, etc) directed towards a person or a group of people repeatedly (i.e. not once-off)?

    Yes

    +

  • B
    Would it be pretty obvious to most people that what's being said or done is victimising, humiliating or threatening?

    Yes

    =

    If the answer to BOTH of these questions is Yes, then the situation could be bullying.
